In brief

Sony emerged from bombed-out postwar Tokyo as Masaru Ibuka's haven for engineers and Akio Morita's mission to transform “Made in Japan” from a mark of cheapness into global quality. The company repeatedly commercialized frontier technology—magnetic tape, transistor radios, Trinitron televisions, compact discs, and the Walkman—by pairing Ibuka's engineering ambition with Morita's product instinct, branding, and cultural fluency. At its peak, Sony did not merely sell electronics; it taught consumers to expect entirely new categories of personal experience.

Its later history reveals the limits of that formula. Betamax's defeat encouraged Sony to buy CBS Records and Columbia Pictures, but owning content rarely improved its devices and conglomerate complexity diluted the brand. The great exception was PlayStation: after Nintendo publicly abandoned their partnership, Sony backed Ken Kutaragi, used CDs and PC-based development to court neglected third-party studios, subsidized hardware, and captured licensing economics. Gaming became Sony's strongest platform, while electronics shifted from an overarching source of Power to one capable business among many.

Five key insights

  1. Engineering joy can precede a product ideaIbuka founded a workplace where engineers could pursue technology and social obligation before knowing what to sell. Failed rice cookers and blankets gave way to radio repair, tape recorders, and eventually category-defining products because the institution was organized around technical curiosity and user benefit.
  2. Brand discipline created global pricing powerMorita rejected Bulova's enormous 100,000-radio order because it required replacing Sony's unknown name. That short-term sacrifice helped build a brand synonymous with innovation and quality across languages, enabling premium prices and changing Western perceptions of Japanese manufacturing.
  3. Superior formats can lose ecosystem warsBetamax offered excellent technology and pioneered time shifting, but its short recording time and antagonism with Hollywood helped MCA and Matsushita rally content behind VHS. Technical performance mattered less than licensing, available programming, participant incentives, and control of the broader value chain.
  4. Content ownership did not guarantee device synergyCBS Records was an excellent standalone financial acquisition, producing billions in annual cash flow decades later. Columbia Pictures was far more expensive and did not give Sony's hardware durable leverage; combining “hardware and software” sounded coherent without creating a shared customer experience or operating model.
  5. PlayStation won by serving complementorsSony used CD capacity, inexpensive PC development tools, generous third-party relations, and loss-leading hardware to attract developers Nintendo undervalued. The resulting network grew from zero first-party franchises to 8,000 games and more than 100 million consoles, making licensing rather than hardware the economic center.
