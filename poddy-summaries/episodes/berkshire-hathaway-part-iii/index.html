In brief

Berkshire Hathaway Part III examines how extraordinary compounding makes further outperformance harder. From 1993 onward, Berkshire needed multibillion-dollar investments while the internet shifted value toward businesses Warren Buffett considered outside his competence. He rejected dot-com excess but missed Microsoft and other durable platforms, then used richly valued Berkshire shares to buy General Re—a troubled insurer whose hands-off management produced losses and scandal. MidAmerican Energy and BNSF fared better, adding regulated businesses and future CEO Greg Abel.

Berkshire's balance sheet regained its advantage during the financial crisis, when scarce trust let Buffett demand double-digit coupons, warrants, and downside protection; a later Bank of America deal generated roughly $26 billion. Succession hires Todd Combs and Ted Weschler helped unlock Apple, turning $36 billion into a stake worth about $120 billion. Yet IBM, Kraft Heinz, and Precision Castparts exposed stale pattern matching. Berkshire's future depends on decentralized operations, disciplined buybacks, and institutional capital allocation after Buffett's halo disappears.

Five key insights

  1. Scale imposes investment gravityA wonderful small acquisition cannot move a company with hundreds of billions in capital. Berkshire's success narrowed its feasible opportunity set to visible public securities and enormous businesses, reducing informational advantage and pulling returns toward the market even when individual decisions were excellent.
  2. Liquidity earns an option premiumDuring 2008, Berkshire's $37 billion cash reserve and reputation mattered more than headline asset values. Companies needing immediate certainty paid high coupons and granted warrants. Conservatively held capital looked inefficient before the crisis, then became a scarce product with exceptional pricing power.
  3. Decentralization requires the right managersBuffett's hands-off model works when acquired leaders are trustworthy owner-operators; applied mechanically to General Re's incumbent management, it preserved hidden underwriting and compliance problems. Autonomy is not governance avoidance—it is a reward for managers whose incentives and judgment have been validated.
  4. Technology changes the meaning of durabilityBuffett avoided technology because rapid change made future cash flows hard to forecast, yet software platforms created some of the strongest modern moats. IBM mistook incumbent relationships for permanence; Apple succeeded because consumer behavior, ecosystem lock-in, and brand made technology economically predictable.
  5. Succession must replace a systemBuffett combined operating authority, insurance judgment, public investing, acquisitions, reputation, and final capital allocation in one person. Berkshire divides those roles among Greg Abel, Ajit Jain, Todd Combs, and Ted Weschler, creating capable specialists but also potential fiefdoms and contested allocation after the founder's halo fades.
