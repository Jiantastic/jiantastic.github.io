In brief

Ben and David argue that Lockheed, the federal government's largest contractor, was forged by existential threat: World War II and the Cold War drove tiny, empowered teams — Kelly Johnson's Skunk Works and the secret Missiles and Space division — to near-impossible feats like a jet fighter prototype in 143 days and the U-2 in eighteen months. Most of what they built were deterrents designed never to be used, and the same culture seeded Silicon Valley itself.

The defining tension is decline from ferocity to protected stasis. The company that delivered the U-2 for $3.5 million became, after the 1993 'Last Supper' forced consolidation, one of five primes living on cost-plus contracts from a single customer — margins capped near 8%, programs spread across 46 states so Congress cannot say no. The hosts conclude there is no market here at all, and the Skunk Works mindset now lives in startups instead.

Five key insights

  1. Existential threat, not process, made Skunk WorksKelly Johnson's handpicked team built the P-80 jet prototype in 143 days in a rented circus tent and delivered the U-2 in about eighteen months for $3.5 million. His 14 rules — viciously restricted headcount, total program authority, designers steps from the shop floor — worked because World War II and the Cold War supplied motivation no management system can manufacture.
  2. Lockheed, not HP, built early Silicon ValleyLockheed Missiles and Space moved into Stanford Industrial Park in 1955 and employed 30,000 people by the mid-60s — ten times Hewlett-Packard's 3,000. It built Sunnyvale from a town of under 10,000, bought from the new chip startups, and employed Jerry Wozniak, which is why Steve Wozniak, and therefore Apple, grew up in the Valley.
  3. Spy satellites quietly made the Blackbird redundantThe first Corona mission in August 1960 produced more Soviet photo coverage than five years of U-2 flights combined, returning film in buckets caught midair by C-130s. David argues the celebrated SR-71 was partly a decoy, and LMSC was the real crown jewel: from 1960 to 1972 it generated 128% of Lockheed's profits while the rest of the company lost money.
  4. Cost-plus contracting removed both risk and marketAfter the Pentagon's 1993 'Last Supper' dinner explicitly ordered contractors to merge, 17 companies collapsed into Lockheed Martin, which now gets about 75% of its $66 billion revenue from one customer at contractually capped ~8% net margins. The hosts' Seven Powers analysis fails outright because with a single buyer and rotating awards, there is no market — only power against new entrants.
  5. Pork-barrel economics guarantee billion-dollar airplanesThe F-22 program shrank from 750 planned planes to 187 delivered while the $62 billion cost stayed fixed, reaching roughly $360 million per aircraft. Building the F-35 across 46 states with 95,000 jobs turns Congress into 538 principal-agent problems that reliably vote yes, confirming Norm Augustine's prediction of ever-costlier planes.
