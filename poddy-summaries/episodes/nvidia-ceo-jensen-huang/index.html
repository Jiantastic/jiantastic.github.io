In brief

Sitting with Jensen Huang at NVIDIA headquarters, this interview argues that NVIDIA's trillion-dollar position is not serial gambling but a repeatable method: simulate the future before betting on it, position the company near opportunities a decade before markets exist, and refuse to be defined as a chip company. From the Riva 128 to CUDA to Mellanox, every famous bet-the-company move was de-risked in advance.

The arc runs from 1997, when a nearly broke NVIDIA taped out an emulated chip sight unseen, to today's AI crucible with sky-high expectations. The tension is between Jensen's calculated confidence and his raw closing admission: building NVIDIA was a million times harder than expected, he wouldn't start it again knowing the cost, and the entrepreneur's real superpower is tricking yourself into asking how hard can it be.

Five key insights

  1. Bet the company only after simulating everythingWith six months of cash, NVIDIA bought a shutting-down emulation company's inventory, ran the entire software stack against a virtual Riva 128, and went straight to production. Jensen's lesson: betting the farm means prefetching future risk into the present, not gambling.
  2. Prediction without causality unlocks enormous marketsAfter AlexNet, Jensen reasoned deep learning was a universal function approximator: for toothpaste choices, social feeds, or weather, you only need prediction, not causal understanding. That implied almost every piece of software would eventually be built this way, changing how chips and computers are designed.
  3. The data center bet began 17 years earlyGeForce Now, remote enterprise graphics, and CUDA supercomputers were three successive data center products that taught NVIDIA the business before AI demand existed. Separating where computing happens from where it's enjoyed removed the one-GPU-per-desktop ceiling on the company's market.
  4. AI inverts hyperscale, so Mellanox was essentialHyperscale virtualizes many users onto commodity machines over Ethernet; AI training distributes one job across millions of processors, so supercomputing-grade networking defines the data center rather than the processor chip. Jensen calls the acquisition one of his best strategic decisions ever.
  5. CUDA compatibility is the only unnegotiable ruleEvery NVIDIA chip across 30 years is architecturally compatible, going back to the original UDA of 1993's Direct NVIDIA plan. Gaming chips sold to run DirectX quietly built an installed base of 250-300 million CUDA GPUs that made the compute platform viable.
