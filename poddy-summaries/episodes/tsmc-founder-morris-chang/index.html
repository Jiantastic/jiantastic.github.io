In brief

Ben and David interview 93-year-old TSMC founder Morris Chang in Taipei, drawing on his untranslated second memoir. The episode's core argument: TSMC became the world's most important chipmaker because it mastered learning-curve economics and, uniquely among leading-edge foundries, never competes with its customers — a trust advantage that won NVIDIA in 1997, landed Apple in 2010, and ultimately beat Intel despite Intel's superior reputation.

The central arc is Chang's 2009 return as CEO. He reversed a layoff of roughly 700 employees, settled a 40-nanometer quality dispute with NVIDIA for over $100 million within 48 hours, then tripled capital spending on 28 nanometers over board objections. That set up the bet-the-company Apple relationship — a 20-nanometer detour that let Samsung snatch Apple's first 16-nanometer orders before TSMC won the business back.

Five key insights

  1. Fixed 8% R&D budget powered the 28-nanometer betChang ended annual budget haggling by fixing R&D at 8% of revenue, recession or not. Emboldened researchers called 28nm 'the sweet spot,' he tripled capex to roughly $6 billion in 2010 over director objections, and the node collided perfectly with the smartphone era.
  2. TSMC deliberately took only half of Apple's demandApple's 20-nanometer request arrived right after the 28nm spending surge, so Chang financed it with corporate bonds rather than cutting the dividend that a third of shareholders depended on — and committed to just half the volume Apple asked for. Apple's purchasing staff said 'you must be crazy,' but Jeff Williams accepted.
  3. Customer trust, not technology, beat Intel for AppleIn February 2011 Intel's CEO persuaded Tim Cook to pause TSMC talks for two months. A month later Cook told Chang there was nothing to worry about because 'Intel just does not know how to be a foundry' — Chang knew Taiwanese PC makers neither liked nor trusted Intel.
  4. Performance-rating layoffs destroy credibility and rarely save moneyThe 600–700 cuts rested on subjective ratings from as many supervisors, and protesters camped near Chang's home. His math: separation pay costs about half a year and training a replacement takes another half year, so if you would rehire within a year you should never lay off.
  5. Learning-curve pricing makes chip manufacturing a natural monopolyChang refined BCG's experience curve with Bill Bain at Texas Instruments: price toward future costs, seize volume, and ride costs down faster than rivals. With fabs costing $20 billion and heading toward $100 billion, only the highest-volume player can keep reinvesting — one winner was structurally inevitable.
