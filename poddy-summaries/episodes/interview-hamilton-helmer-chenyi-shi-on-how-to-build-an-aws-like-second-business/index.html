In brief

Hamilton Helmer and Chenyi Shi extend the Seven Powers framework from defending one successful business to deciding where a company should expand next. Their rule is sequential: understand the core business's power precisely, expand first beneath that existing power umbrella, and only then attempt a genuinely new business by reusing capabilities for a different customer need.

The tension is that celebrated second acts such as AWS are inventions, not automatic extensions of the original moat. They can create enormous value, but Amazon's Fire Phone and Alexa losses show the odds. The framework therefore separates low-risk power extension from higher-risk coaction, while emphasizing that no process replaces entrepreneurial judgment, experimentation, and failure.

Five key insights

  1. Product-market fit does not create durabilityBowmar calculators grew from roughly $3 million to $100 million yet disappeared, while the iPod became the foundation for Apple's next era. Seven Powers addresses the second invention: the economic structure that lets attractive profits persist after innovation succeeds.
  2. Expand beneath the existing power umbrellaNetflix could spread content costs across Korea, while Uber entering China had to rebuild local rider-driver density from scratch. The same-looking geographic expansion can therefore be low-risk scale extension for one company and an entirely new business for another.
  3. Coaction produces most successful second businessesHelmer's S&P 100 work attributes roughly 90% of value from new businesses to coaction: applying partly shared skills to a different customer need. AWS, PlayStation, the iPhone, and 3M's Post-it Note fit better than unrelated diversification because each starts with useful capabilities without pretending the old moat transfers.
  4. A repeatable innovation process is suspiciousThe guests call a rigid seventeen-step innovation system a major red flag. New-business creation is adaptive and power-law distributed; Amazon could produce AWS only while tolerating expensive failures such as Fire Phone and Alexa.
  5. Acquisitions need a power-based advantageThe central M&A question is why an asset is worth more to the buyer than to its better-informed seller. Disney could exploit Marvel, Pixar, and Lucasfilm across its distribution system, while generic headcount synergies are usually offset by the diseconomies of a larger organization.
