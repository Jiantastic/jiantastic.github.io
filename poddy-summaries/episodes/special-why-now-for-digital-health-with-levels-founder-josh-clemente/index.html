In brief

Levels founder Josh Clemente applies aerospace systems thinking to metabolic health. After leading SpaceX Dragon life-support work, chronic stress, poor sleep, and fatigue pushed him to investigate how his body produced energy. Finger-prick measurements were too sparse; a continuous glucose monitor revealed large responses to supposedly healthy foods. Levels pairs Abbott or Dexcom sensors with prescriptions, fulfillment, meal and activity logging, and an insight layer that translates glucose curves into personal experiments and behavior change for people without diabetes.

The company argues medicine measures glucose after dysfunction appears, while prevention requires feedback during everyday choices. A month-long direct-to-consumer program establishes a baseline, tests food, exercise, and sleep, then builds individualized habits. Cash pay avoids enterprise procurement and forces a premium experience; research and consumer data can later support employer or insurance coverage. Levels' defensibility rests on actionable metrics, longitudinal lifestyle-linked data, and personalization. Cheaper or noninvasive sensors would expand rather than erase its software opportunity.

Five key insights

  1. Measure systems before they failTraditional access treats a CGM as scarce therapy after diabetes, but aerospace engineering instruments healthy systems to detect failure modes early. Continuous metabolic feedback makes prevention observable instead of waiting for fasting labs or symptoms after compensation mechanisms are already breaking down.
  2. Personal data beats generic nutrition adviceOatmeal, sweet potatoes, rice, sleep loss, and exercise affect individuals differently. Seeing one's own curve makes a short walk after a meal more persuasive than population guidance because the action and physiological response are connected immediately and objectively.
  3. Insight captures more value than sensingAbbott and Dexcom solve difficult measurement hardware, but raw glucose traces do not explain what to change. Levels combines curve shape, meal context, activity, and history into scores and recommendations. Its core product is the delta between accurate data and sustained behavior change.
  4. Premium consumers can open reimbursementCash pay supplies demand evidence, margins, rapid feedback, and a quality bar unconstrained by enterprise buyers. If research shows improved risk markers and hardware costs decline, employers and insurers can fund access later without starting from a low-engagement reimbursement product.
  5. Continuous feedback decentralizes healthcarePopulation averages cannot capture multivariate individual response. Large anonymized data sets can identify metabolic phenotypes, but the final actor remains the person making daily choices. Personalized loops may improve population outcomes without one universal diet or policy.
