In brief

Google became the greatest business of all time not because it was the first search engine but because it was the last: PageRank delivered dramatically better relevance, home-built commodity infrastructure made serving results radically cheap, and an auction-based ad model matched high-intent queries to advertisers so efficiently that Alphabet now generates more profit than any other U.S. company.

The episode's central arc is the search for a business model: for years Google had adoring users and exploding costs but almost no revenue, and incumbents rejected its technology because relevance destroyed banner-ad page views. Salvation came from copying Overture's pay-per-click auction, improving it with click-through relevance and second-price mechanics, then betting the company on a $100 million AOL guarantee that turned scale into rising revenue per search.

Five key insights

  1. Google's revenue per search rises with scaleMore advertisers bidding on each keyword improves price discovery and monetizes long-tail queries, so scale raises revenue per query even as unit costs fall. That let Google offer distribution partners revenue shares of 85 to even 100 percent that no competitor could economically match.
  2. Portals rejected PageRank to protect page viewsExcite's CEO killed a roughly $1 million license after a side-by-side demo because relevant results sent users away from his site, destroying CPM banner impressions. Yahoo likewise treated search as good-enough backfill for its hand-curated directory, which is why Google ultimately beat them all.
  3. Commodity hardware enabled 85 percent gross marginsGoogle mounted caseless motherboards on corkboard, leased data-center space by square footage, and tolerated hardware failure rates over 10 percent by replicating everything three to five times in software. Next door, competitor Inktomi paid for gleaming enterprise Sun servers.
  4. AdWords was Overture's model plus relevanceGoTo pioneered pay-per-click keyword auctions and did $100 million in first-year revenue; Google grafted on click-through rate (AdRank) and second-price auctions, mathematically maximizing expected revenue while rewarding better ads with cheaper clicks. Google later paid Overture $360 million for patent infringement.
  5. The AOL guarantee was bet-the-company leverageIn 2002 Google guaranteed AOL $100 million it did not have, plus an 85 percent revenue share and warrants; Sergey Brin admitted 'we could have gone bankrupt.' AOL instead earned $35 million in the first half-year and $200 million in 2003, making Google the major paid-search player overnight.
