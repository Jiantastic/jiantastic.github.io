In brief

Eric Ries argues that short-termism is not a law of nature but a flaw in human-made financial infrastructure — and infrastructure can be rebuilt. The Long-Term Stock Exchange, SEC-approved as only the fifth national securities exchange, operationalizes that belief: companies make binding, enforceable pledges to long-term principles, long-tenured shareholders can earn superior voting rights or dividends, and the exchange earns money by serving companies rather than traders.

The arc traces how Ries repeatedly backs polarizing ideas that later become consensus — a test reader begged him to cut the exchange idea from The Lean Startup, just as VCs once yelled at him for describing continuous deployment. The unresolved tension is whether LTSE can survive entrenched incumbents who profit from the status quo and persuade any company to list first, when maybe everybody would like to go second.

Five key insights

  1. Broken LTSE pledges become securities violationsListed companies must register binding operational commitments for each long-term principle, and violating one carries real enforcement penalties. This makes the standards self-selecting: only companies genuinely serious about long-termism would rationally sign up, unlike aspirational S-1 language investors ignore.
  2. Long-term holders can earn superior voting rightsRies's middle ground on dual-class stock — which he compares to feudalism when perpetual — lets investors earn into the privileged class through holding tenure, via vote multipliers or progressive dividends. LTSE software tracks long-term ownership in real time, so rewards adjust automatically when holders trade out.
  3. Algorithmic and passive trading drained price informationMost trades are now machines hitting buttons and much ownership sits in index funds, so far fewer active human voices set prices and a single activist can drag passive money with them. One quant with a ten-minute average holding period still had to vote proxies — governance assigned to someone who says shorting cattle futures doesn't bother the cows.
  4. Staying private longer follows from policy choicesRies reframes a VC's case for staying private — unlimited capital, any terms, no disclosure — as a bit field of switches that flip when conditions change, citing a thermostatic equilibrium between private and public markets. WeWork proved the point: its last-gasp rescue attempt was an IPO, and undisclosed secondary transactions echo the fraud his grandparents' Depression-era rules were built to stop.
  5. LTSE sells software to companies, not tradesInstead of per-trade take rates that reward volume, LTSE monetizes tools like captable.io, hiringplan.io, and startuprunway.io from seed through IPO, plus services such as placing large blocks with long-term investors after market close. This inverts the incumbent model where investors are the customer and companies are merely 'issuers.'
