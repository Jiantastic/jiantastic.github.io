In brief

Ethereum began when teenage Bitcoin writer Vitalik Buterin saw that separate blockchain projects could become one programmable base layer. His 2013 white paper specified a decentralized virtual machine whose smart contracts could represent currencies, organizations, markets, and applications. A distributed founding group formed a Swiss foundation, funded development through an Ether crowdsale, and launched in 2015. Unlike Bitcoin's narrow monetary design, Ethereum became an evolving ‘world computer’ shaped by developers, token holders, miners, and social consensus.

Ethereum's earliest killer application was fundraising: ERC-20 tokens powered an ICO boom that bootstrapped liquidity, users, and infrastructure before collapsing. The DAO hack forced a human choice to reverse theft through a hard fork, leaving Ethereum Classic. CryptoKitties introduced NFTs; Maker, Uniswap, and Compound assembled decentralized finance. Ethereum's developer lead, standards, liquidity, and legitimacy create switching costs, but congestion, gas fees, slow governance, competing chains, regulation, and the hazardous transition toward proof of stake threaten its promise.

Five key insights

  1. General platforms absorb specialized productsButerin noticed that colored coins, domain systems, and financial protocols were each adding narrow logic to Bitcoin. A programmable virtual machine moved that logic into applications, letting one network support uses its creators could not anticipate and turning developer creativity into the product roadmap.
  2. Legitimacy is a network assetEthereum and Ethereum Classic share ancestry and compatible technology, yet their values diverged because developers, exchanges, applications, and users coordinated around one chain. History, visible stewardship, standards, and collective belief compound into brand and switching costs that a technically similar fork cannot copy.
  3. Speculation can bootstrap useful infrastructureICOs brought dubious projects and severe losses, but also created wallets, exchanges, token standards, capital, and a global user base. Like Silk Road's role in Bitcoin, an initially problematic use case financed and stress-tested infrastructure later used for NFTs, lending, and decentralized exchange.
  4. Immutability ultimately includes human consensusThe DAO exploit followed deployed code, yet the community chose to fork and restore funds. Ethereum therefore combines cryptographic execution with weak subjectivity: software determines state until enough participants agree that exceptional circumstances justify changing the rules.
  5. Success can degrade a shared networkMore applications and users increased Ethereum's economic value but also drove gas fees above practical levels and reduced utility. Network effects coexist with diseconomies when every node processes the same state. Scaling must preserve security and composability without destroying the legitimacy that created the lead.
