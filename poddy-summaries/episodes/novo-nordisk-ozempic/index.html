In brief

Novo Nordisk became Europe's most valuable company by staying obsessively focused on one interrelated field—metabolic disease—for a full century. What began as insulin for type 1 diabetes became the GLP-1 drugs Ozempic and Wegovy. A nonprofit foundation that still controls the company, not a diversified pharma strategy, is what made that century of single-minded focus possible.

The defining tension is between short-term commercial logic and the foundation's mandate for stability. For over a decade, management tried to sell into pharma megamergers; in 2004 the foundation board vetoed a deal, forcing independence. That single decision preserved the small internal GLP-1 program that became semaglutide—meaning the ownership structure, not managerial vision, is ultimately why Ozempic exists at all.

Five key insights

  1. Foundation control saved the company from acquisitionIn 2004 management had a deal to merge with Switzerland's Serono, but the controlling foundation board rejected it, pointing to 15 years of 20%-plus compounding growth. Without that veto, the small internal program that produced Ozempic would likely have been dissolved.
  2. Bitter local rivalry drove a century of innovationThe Peterson brothers, fired after a dispute, founded Novo down the street from Nordisk, and the two hated each other for 65 years. That competition produced shelf-stable liquid insulin, NPH, and the insulin pen before they finally merged in 1989.
  3. GLP-1's short half-life stalled it for decadesNative GLP-1 breaks down in about five minutes, so the industry abandoned it as a drug candidate. Lotte Bjerre Knudsen grafted a fatty acid onto the molecule so it binds albumin, extending the half-life to 13 hours and enabling a once-daily drug.
  4. A 10% weight-loss threshold defined the marketLiraglutide (Saxenda) delivered only about an 8% BMI reduction and flopped commercially. Semaglutide crossed 15%, past the informal threshold where patients and doctors judge a weight-loss drug worth the trouble, which is why it became a blockbuster while its predecessor did not.
  5. Middlemen sever the healthcare demand signalA patient's drug passes through manufacturer, distributor, pharmacy, PBM, insurer, and employer, with rebates reaching 75% of sticker price. Because insurers churn patients every ~3.7 years, they have no incentive to fund long-term preventive drugs, leaving Medicare and taxpayers to hold the bag.
