In brief

This episode argues that Microsoft's real invention was not any single product but the software-only business model itself: zero-marginal-cost code, licensed per machine to hardware makers, riding two 'freak laws of nature' — Moore's Law and software's infinite replicability. Gates and Allen saw exponential cheapening of compute meant 'a computer on every desk and in every home,' and positioned Microsoft as the point of integration an entire industry would have to pay.

The central arc is Microsoft's climb from dependent little brother to emperor. A capped, exclusive MITS contract and rampant piracy taught Gates to control distribution; the fixed-fee IBM deal that retained DOS rights let clones make Microsoft rich; and 'riding the bear' through OS/2 ended when Windows 3.0 — the unloved plan B — took off, letting Microsoft pass IBM in market cap by 1993 and stage the glorious Windows 95 coronation.

Five key insights

  1. Fixed-fee IBM deal, but Microsoft kept DOSMicrosoft took only $430,000 total from IBM with no royalties, but retained the right to license DOS to anyone on any terms. When Compaq and the clones reverse-engineered the BIOS and built to the same spec, every one of them paid Microsoft per machine — IBM generated the demand, Microsoft captured the value.
  2. Piracy forced the per-machine OEM licensing modelMITS sold thousands of Altairs monthly while only a few hundred copies of BASIC sold, because hobbyists copied it freely — software wasn't even clearly copyrightable until Congress amended Title 17 in 1980. Gates concluded software must be bundled into the hardware maker's purchase price, the royalty model that built the company.
  3. Cheap BASIC licenses bought the industry standardGates deliberately underpriced deals — Apple got eight years of BASIC for $31,000 — so every manufacturer bundled Microsoft's version rather than writing a rival. His 'success reinforces success' spiral worked because five employees carried almost no fixed cost, letting Microsoft play for standard-setting instead of near-term revenue.
  4. Hedged bets beat conviction during platform shiftsMicrosoft simultaneously toed the IBM OS/2 party line, built Excel first for the Macintosh, and kept a 65-person misfit team on Windows. When Windows 3.0 was unexpectedly loved, an executive offsite flipped plan B to plan A — a pivot possible only because every outcome had a Microsoft product waiting.
  5. Gates still owned 49 percent at IPOThe only dilution ever was 5% to TVI at a $20 million post-money — roughly 1x revenue — plus Ballmer's 8.5% and a small option pool. Microsoft never spent its $45 million IPO proceeds; that capital efficiency gave Gates founder control to bet the company repeatedly, unlike any modern venture-backed firm.
