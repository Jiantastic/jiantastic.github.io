In brief

Steve Ballmer — worth $130 billion eleven years after leaving Microsoft, almost entirely from holding his stock — returns to respond to Acquired's Microsoft series, arguing the company's success came from deliberately built capabilities: the enterprise business he fathered out of fear of IBM, the recurring-revenue enterprise agreement, and Azure. Great companies, he insists, are at best two-trick ponies, and Microsoft's two tricks — desktop and enterprise — both had to be carried into the cloud.

The through-line is Microsoft's relationship with its own franchise: decades spent riding the bear of IBM gave way to a fear-plus-overconfidence instinct to put Windows everywhere, which doomed phone and search even as it worked for servers. That tension ran through Ballmer's fraying partnership with Bill Gates — a year of silence, the Longhorn mistake of mistakes, hardware fights — and ended when the board rejected his phone plan and he resigned, staying loyal by never selling.

Five key insights

  1. Just holding Microsoft grew $20B to $130BBallmer's one investment decision since 2014 was keeping substantially all his Microsoft stock, on the logic that selling only wins if Microsoft underperforms the index by roughly the capital-gains rate. Dividend checks alone roughly cover his family's near-billion-dollar annual philanthropy.
  2. Non-exclusive DOS licensing was the pivotal luckMicrosoft paid about $45,000-$49,000 to Seattle Computer Products for an operating system it didn't yet own, then licensed it to IBM one-time and non-exclusively. When Compaq and other clones standardized on DOS, Microsoft — not IBM — became the ecosystem's point of integration.
  3. Enterprise agreements fixed a halving-revenue problemUnder Select licensing, upgrades cost less than half the price of new licenses, so Microsoft's revenue base was structurally shrinking. The three-year, per-machine, everything-included enterprise agreement created recurring revenue and sold IT departments peace of mind, like an insurance policy.
  4. Microsoft forced Windows where it didn't belongBallmer rejects the claim that Microsoft stuck with Windows too long; the mistake was Windows-izing the phone's UI and processor choice out of combined fear and overconfidence. Only two phone models won — Apple's hardware-profit model and Android's ad-monetized giveaway — and Microsoft missed Verizon's window to Android around Christmas 2008.
  5. Satya's ego-free Chi Lu offer sealed successionDuring the recruiting meeting, Satya Nadella proposed hiring Chi Lu as his own boss, inverting the reporting chain on the spot. Ballmer read that as proof Satya would put the company first, then rotated him into Server and Tools to build the experience needed to become CEO.
