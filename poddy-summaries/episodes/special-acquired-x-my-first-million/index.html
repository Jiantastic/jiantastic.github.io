In brief

Shaan Puri's Bebo story shows how to recover value after a celebrated internet brand has already failed. Michael and Xochi Birch sell Bebo to AOL for $850 million, repurchase its bankrupt remnants for $1 million, and discover that the famous domain and 80-million-address email list matter less than expected. The team preserves SEO cheaply, uses self-aware nostalgia to attract one million fresh signups, tests multiple products sequentially, and turns live-streaming infrastructure into a high-school esports platform sold to Twitch.

The second half applies My First Million's opportunity-finding style. The hosts distinguish venture moonshots from cash-flow entrepreneurship, map founder personalities needed at successive stages, and brainstorm workation Wi-Fi data, a SPAC index, climate infrastructure, crypto services, solar financing, and small-business acquisition. The recurring method is to identify a newly acute need, start with a narrow wedge, exploit underused distribution or financing, and match one's existing skill—engineering, audience, growth, or operations—to a business where it creates asymmetric improvement.

Five key insights

  1. Distressed brands are option portfoliosBebo's recognizable name, domain, search rankings, user data, code, and litigation rights each had different value and decay rates. The legal claim unexpectedly outperformed the email list, demonstrating why buyers should underwrite assets separately rather than rely on headline nostalgia.
  2. Preserve attention while delaying the product betThe team freezes profile pages to retain search traffic and launches a humorous founder-return video before knowing what Bebo will become. That creates one million permissioned new signups, which can be segmented into controlled product tests without maintaining the costly legacy network.
  3. Usage intensity does not guarantee growthBlab's casual users stayed for hours but came to make new friends, so they did not invite existing ones; celebrity broadcasters attracted audiences but appeared only weekly. Neither cohort supplied both habit and acquisition, making impressive engagement insufficient for a sustainable network.
  4. Selling requires buyer discoveryPuri interviews potential acquirers about their top strategic priorities, then removes everything from Bebo's story except the relevant solution. Twitch needs scalable original esports content, an experienced leader, engineers, code, and accumulated learning immediately—assets that cannot simply be recruited one by one.
  5. Buy where your skill changes outcomesProfitable small businesses can use SBA and seller financing, avoiding the market-risk lottery of a new startup. A buyer with a proven acquisition channel, product expansion ability, or operational expertise can identify neglected assets and create value through concrete improvements rather than multiple expansion alone.
