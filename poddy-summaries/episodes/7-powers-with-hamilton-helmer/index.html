In brief

Hamilton Helmer explains why a large market and a good product do not by themselves create an enduring company. Power is a material business-model benefit paired with a barrier that prevents capable competitors from copying away the returns. His seven exhaustive categories—scale economies, network economies, counter-positioning, switching costs, branding, cornered resource, and process power—connect strategy directly to long-term value. Because most valuation lies years ahead, understanding persistence matters more than extrapolating current growth.

Power is usually established during a market's takeoff, when technology, customers, and business models remain fluid; once the window closes, incumbents cannot easily pivot and late entrants face accumulated scale, networks, or switching costs. The framework is therefore a decision tool for founders, not a retrospective moat checklist. Helmer stresses nuance: disruption can destroy value without capturing it, network effects can plateau, switching costs can be spent away through acquisition, counter-positioning is temporary without another power, and great leadership is necessary but rarely sufficient.

Five key insights

  1. Power requires benefit and barrierA benefit improves the company's economics materially; a barrier keeps motivated, capable rivals from taking it away. Product improvements without defensibility are competed out, while barriers without customer or economic benefit protect nothing valuable. Both conditions must hold against every relevant competitor.
  2. Takeoff is the strategic windowPower forms while a category is changing quickly and founders still have degrees of freedom over product, market, and business model. Network economies, scale economies, and switching costs compound with size, so entering several years behind a similar company can become fatal when the market consolidates.
  3. Counter-positioning exploits incumbent self-harmA challenger adopts a superior model that an incumbent cannot copy without damaging an existing profit pool, incentives, or identity. Netflix's no-late-fee subscription attacked half of Blockbuster's income. Yet other startups can imitate the challenger, so durable value usually requires a second power.
  4. Claimed network effects need economic mappingNetworks may be local, asymmetric, nonlinear, or able to support several efficient competitors. Uber's density benefit can flatten once multiple services provide adequate liquidity. Strategy requires mapping who values whom, how strongly, at what scale, and whether that advantage actually becomes monetizable profit.
  5. Power guides expansion after successAct-two growth should remain under the existing power umbrella. Uber's local density largely resets by geography, while Netflix can spread fixed content costs across countries wherever programming travels. Expansion creates value when the original benefit and barrier extend to new customers rather than merely increasing corporate scope.
