In brief

The familiar story — that Sega died from Japanese management blunders, the harebrained 32X, and a botched surprise Saturn launch — is incomplete. Ben and David argue Sega was fundamentally an arcade giant, and its console flailing was a panicked but semi-rational response to Sony's PlayStation, which simultaneously invaded the living room and destroyed the arcade business that was Sega's real profit engine.

The episode's arc is a deliberate retelling. First comes the Console Wars version, where a jealous Sega of Japan overrules Tom Kalinsky's winning Sega of America; then a more charitable one, where Virtua Fighter teaches Sony that 3D is the future, Namco ships PlayStation hardware into arcades as the System 11 board, and Steve Race's '$299' moment at E3 1995 collapses consumer, retailer, and developer confidence at once.

Five key insights

  1. Console add-ons guarantee a shrinking marketThe console business is razor-and-blades: profits come from software amortized over the largest possible install base, but an add-on caps its market at existing owners. The Sega CD sold roughly 3 million units against 30 million Genesis consoles, so developers skipped it and the platform death-spiraled.
  2. Sega was always an arcade company firstHome consoles were side projects; Sega dominated a global arcade industry then bigger than home gaming, selling cabinets and operating its own arcade chains. Its DNA of iterating proprietary hardware per cabinet explains the add-on obsession and reluctance to commit to five-year console platforms.
  3. Virtua Fighter handed Sony the PlayStation blueprintThe 1993 3D fighter sold over 40,000 cabinets at $10,000-plus each — roughly half a billion dollars — and proved polygonal 3D games worked. Sony said it made the PlayStation's direction 'instantly clear' and built the console explicitly as an arcade killer.
  4. Namco's System 11 put PlayStation inside arcadesSony recruited Namco, Konami, and Midway — arcade makers with no competing console — and shipped PlayStation hardware as the Tekken arcade board in September 1994. Global arcade revenue fell from about $7 billion to $2 billion by decade's end, gutting Sega's cash engine.
  5. One $299 announcement killed the Saturn instantlyAfter Sega's $399 surprise launch at E3 1995, ex-Sega marketer Steve Race walked to the podium for Sony and said only '$299.' Consumers waited four months for the superior machine, KB Toys dropped Sega entirely, and third-party developers canceled Saturn projects.
