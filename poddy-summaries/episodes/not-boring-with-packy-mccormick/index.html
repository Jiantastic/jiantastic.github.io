In brief

Packy McCormick turns a failed pandemic-era social club into Not Boring, a free strategy newsletter that grows from roughly 1,000 subscribers to 88,460 in about 18 months. Instead of placing essays behind a paywall, he maximizes reach, surveys the audience, and sells scarce sponsorships and company deep dives to businesses that value its unusually concentrated population of founders and decision-makers. By late 2021, the one-person media company was approaching its public goal of $1 million in annual revenue.

The more consequential move is Not Boring Capital. Editorial credibility attracts founders, readers become LPs and collaborators, investments produce firsthand material, and AngelList supplies a venture firm's legal and operational back office. The result is a creator-investor flywheel unavailable a decade earlier. Yet its power depends on alignment rather than raw reach: founders care when they already trust the writing. McCormick cannot delegate the core voice easily, and indiscriminate sponsors, products, hiring, or capital would dilute the very independence that creates deal flow.

Five key insights

  1. Free distribution enlarged every later optionMcCormick repeatedly postponed paid subscriptions because even a successful conversion would cap sharing and audience growth. A larger free readership increased sponsorship pricing, founder access, investment sourcing, reputation, and future product flexibility—making attention more valuable than predictable subscription revenue at that stage.
  2. Audience quality matters more than sizeA broad million-person list would not create the same economics. Not Boring attracted technology operators, founders, investors, and budget owners through dense strategy writing. Sponsors value measurable access to that group, while founders value an investment only when prior reading has already established trust.
  3. Sponsored analysis must remain editorially credibleMcCormick aims to publish the best existing explanation of each sponsor, rejects companies that are not interesting to readers, demands access, and holds price because skipping a paid piece returns scarce time. The model works only while the audience believes selection and analysis remain genuine.
  4. Software unbundled the media-investment firmSubstack supplies publishing and email delivery; Twitter supplies discovery; AngelList supplies fundraising, compliance, reporting, and human operational expertise. Those modular systems let one person operate both a media company and a 91-company venture portfolio without recreating the staffs of a publisher or fund.
  5. Personal leverage creates personal fragilityThe creator's voice is the product, a cornered resource, and the bottleneck. Hiring writers risks changing expectations; taking time off slows momentum; selling unrelated products can turn trust into a shill factory; joining a large fund immediately reduces the independent perspective it hoped to acquire.
