In brief

What sounds like lighthearted holiday fare about candy is actually one of the great untold American business epics: Mars is a $50 billion revenue private company that out-earns Coca-Cola, owned entirely by America's second-wealthiest family. The episode argues that Forrest Mars Sr. — reclusive, ruthless, and systems-obsessed — belongs alongside Sam Walton and Henry Ford, having mastered scale economies, modern marketing, and vertical integration decades before those became standard playbooks.

The driving arc is a father-son rupture: denied a one-third stake in Frank Mars's company, Forrest exiles himself to Europe, learns chocolate-making undercover on Nestlé's factory lines, then returns to build M&M's using his chief rival Hershey's own chocolate, capital, and military contracts — before buying back his father's company outright. The tension throughout is Mars's relentless efficiency against Hershey, the complacent trust-owned incumbent that supplied its own destroyer.

Five key insights

  1. First to market sets a nation's tasteHershey's milk chocolate carried a sour note — likely from spoiled milk in his trial-and-error process — yet because he was first at scale, that flavor became what Americans think chocolate is. Nostalgia compounds the lock-in: 75% of candy is eaten by adults reaching back to childhood taste memories.
  2. Forrest built M&M's using Hershey's own resourcesBy giving Hershey president William Murray's son Bruce a 20% stake, Forrest secured chocolate, sugar, capital, and access to the military's exclusive chocolate contract during WWII rationing — from his biggest rival. He then pushed Bruce out in 1949, buying full control for $1 million.
  3. ROTA, not profit, is Mars's master metricFrom T.G. Rose's obscure management textbook, Forrest adopted return on total assets — with assets constantly re-marked to replacement value, not book cost. The 18% target forces every factory investment to pay back in roughly five years, and pairing it with 2-4x market pay keeps output per employee nearly double Hershey's.
  4. Cheaper fillings gave Mars durable price-war advantageSnickers and Milky Way are mostly nougat, caramel, and peanuts sealed in chocolate — far lower cost-of-goods than Hershey's all-chocolate bar for equal perceived value. When cocoa prices spiked, Mars announced bigger bars at lower prices, boxing Hershey in and passing it as America's top candy company in 1973.
  5. Mars is really a pet-care companyPet care generates 59% of Mars's $50 billion revenue and employs roughly 100,000 of its 140,000 people, spanning Pedigree, Whiskas, Royal Canin prescription food, and thousands of Banfield and VCA veterinary hospitals. The candy empire the episode chronicles is now the smaller half of the business.
