In brief

The episode argues that Renaissance Technologies' Medallion fund is proof the market can be beaten: 66% gross annual returns over three decades, with no losing year since 1990. Crucially, it was built by outsiders — codebreaker Jim Simons and PhD scientists with no investing background — who treated markets as a signal-processing problem, using hidden Markov models to trade on statistical patterns while ignoring fundamentals entirely.

The central arc is the 25-year gap between the idea and the machine: from the 1964 IDA paper through gut-driven currency trading, near-blowups, and spinouts, until clean data, one unified model, and leverage made it work. The tension is that the money machine only survives by staying small and secret — kicking out all outside investors, capping fund size against slippage, and engineering incentives so the world's smartest people never leave.

Five key insights

  1. Code breaking and trading are the same problemBoth mine faint signal from apparently random noise using hidden Markov models — predicting future states from observed patterns without understanding underlying causes. Simons and his IDA colleagues laid out the entire Rentech approach in a 1964 paper, twenty years before the firm existed.
  2. A tiny edge, massively repeated, prints billionsMercer's line — 'we're 100% right 50.75% of the time' — captures the model: Kelly-criterion bet sizing across 150,000–300,000 trades a day. Basket options leverage amplified it, letting Medallion control $60 billion of positions on $5 billion of capital in 2002.
  3. One shared model makes collaboration self-interestedUnlike Citadel or multi-strategy funds where pods compete, all of Rentech's ~200 researchers work on a single model and see the whole codebase, so anyone's improvement pays everyone. Combined with a ~14-year median tenure, credit-hoarding loses its logic.
  4. Refusing to scale is what preserved the returnsSlippage — moving the market with your own trades — caps how big the strategy can get. Rentech closed Medallion in 1993, expelled outside LPs from 2003, and kept it near $10–15 billion, sustaining 30%+ gross returns every year while institutional funds absorbed outside demand.
  5. The 44% carry transfers wealth to current staffWith no outside LPs, charging yourself 5-and-44 seems absurd — until you see it as routing roughly half of each year's gains from the tenured investor base to the people currently working on the model, a retention mechanism structured like academic tenure.
