In brief

Google is the textbook innovator's dilemma: its own researchers invented the transformer that powers the entire AI revolution, yet commercializing it threatens a 90%-share search monopoly whose profits AI has not come close to replacing. Ben and David argue Google still holds every strategic AI asset — a frontier model, its own chips, a $50 billion cloud, and the internet's front door — making the race Google's to lose.

The arc is a two-decade talent story: Google assembled nearly every AI pioneer, from Jeff Dean's early language models to Brain and DeepMind, then watched key people leave to found OpenAI and Anthropic after it hesitated to ship. The tension after ChatGPT's code red is whether Sundar can thread the needle — merging labs, standardizing on Gemini, protecting search economics — without caution around cannibalization costing Google the platform shift it made possible.

Five key insights

  1. Every major AI lab traces back to GoogleA decade before ChatGPT, Ilya Sutskever, Dario Amodei, Noam Shazeer, Demis Hassabis, and Mustafa Suleyman were all Google employees. OpenAI only got started when seven researchers were pried loose at the 2015 Rosewood dinner — and most attendees still refused to leave.
  2. The AI era actually began in 2012The cat paper and AlexNet proved neural networks could classify unlabeled data, powering the recommendation feeds of YouTube, Facebook, and TikTok. Those classifiers drove hundreds of billions of dollars in revenue a full decade before chatbots arrived.
  3. Google built ChatGPT first and shelved itNoam Shazeer's Meena chatbot existed years before ChatGPT, but it lacked safety post-training, and replacing ten blue links with direct answers threatened ad revenue and publisher relationships. He quit to found Character AI, and Google later paid roughly $2.7 billion to bring him back.
  4. TPUs make Google the low-cost token producerRivals pay NVIDIA roughly 80% gross margins while Google pays Broadcom about 50%, and chips are over half the cost of an AI data center. With two to three million TPUs and inference approaching a quadrillion tokens per month, that cost edge could decide a market where model margins are thin.
  5. AI monetization still can't match search economicsSearch earns Google roughly $400 per US user per year from a free product, while AI chat currently monetizes through subscriptions few will buy. Longer, higher-intent queries should eventually support better ad rates, but that ad model does not exist yet.
