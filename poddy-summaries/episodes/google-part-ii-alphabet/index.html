In brief

Ben and David argue that Google's seemingly scattered 2000s product spree—Gmail, Maps, Docs, YouTube, Chrome, Android—was one coherent strategy: every free product grew web usage, feeding the search-ads money printer, while building moats against Microsoft's control of the browser and operating system. What Wall Street read as a drunken juggler was an innovation factory protecting the best business model of all time.

The tension runs between bottom-up genius and top-down force. Organic, technically-driven products—Gmail, Maps, Chrome, Android—let Google become the only dominant company to survive a major platform shift, into mobile. The one commanded effort, Google Plus, poisoned the culture, cost Google messaging and cloud, and triggered the unifying Alphabet reorg—just as the company quietly amassed the data and talent that would define the AI era.

Five key insights

  1. Web apps were Google's moat against MicrosoftOver 90% of searches happened in Internet Explorer on Windows, so Google existed at Microsoft's pleasure. Gmail, Maps, and Docs got consumers hooked on browser applications Microsoft couldn't kill without a revolt, and Chrome finished the job—cutting IE from roughly 70% share to 15% by 2014.
  2. Android's less-than-free model won mobileWhere Microsoft charged OEMs for Windows Mobile, Google gave Android away open source and paid carriers and handset makers a search revenue share. Market share went from about 5% in late 2009 to 80% by 2013, keeping Google search default on billions of devices.
  3. YouTube regraded from C to A+Bought for $1.65 billion in stock plus roughly $5 billion of operating losses, YouTube now does over $50 billion in revenue—more than Netflix and second only to Disney among media companies—with an estimated $8 billion in operating income and a $500 billion standalone valuation.
  4. Successful Google products embed a core technical insightPageRank, Gmail's Ajax, real-time collaboration in Docs, and Chrome's V8 engine each turned a technology breakthrough directly into the user experience; Eric Schmidt refused to fund projects without one. Google Plus, Wave, and Buzz had no such insight and all failed.
  5. Google Plus's real cost was missed opportunitiesThe top-down, bonus-tied social push was Google's Vista: it soured the culture and distracted leadership, plausibly costing Google messaging (WhatsApp) and years of cloud investment. Between Plus and Gemini, the hosts can't name a single breakthrough consumer product Google shipped.
