In brief

JavaScript creator and former Netscape and Mozilla architect Brendan Eich traces the browser's recurring struggle between open access and platform control. Netscape made the web commercially safe with SSL but inherited cookies and embedded resources that enabled invisible third-party tracking. Microsoft killed Netscape through Windows bundling; Mozilla preserved its code through open source and Firefox briefly reopened competition; then Google's search funding, distribution, and Chrome rebuilt browser dominance around an advertising company whose incentives increasingly favored identity and data collection.

Brave is Eich's attempt to restore user power without breaking web compatibility. Built on Chromium, it blocks trackers by default, improving speed, battery, and data use while retaining Chrome extensions. Opt-in private ads match locally and return 70% of revenue through Basic Attention Token, connecting users, advertisers, and creators without centralized behavioral dossiers. Brave's wallet and multichain ambitions extend that model toward user-owned identity and payments, but Eich remains pragmatic: servers, custodians, regulation, and Web2 will persist; cryptography should improve their bargains rather than pretend they disappear.

Five key insights

  1. Browser history repeats through distribution powerInternet Explorer beat Netscape by tying itself to Windows; Chrome used Google's reach and payments; Safari and Edge retain operating-system privilege. Technical quality matters, but mass-market browsers repeatedly require default placement, compatibility, and distribution that independent challengers struggle to finance.
  2. Backward compatibility preserves both value and harmThe web's accreted content makes incompatible improvement nearly impossible. Cookies and third-party embeds could not be removed once sites depended on them, so mechanisms created for first-party utility evolved into an advertising surveillance layer that browsers must now counter without breaking pages.
  3. Privacy creates economic bargaining powerTracking is not merely an abstract personal-rights issue: uncontrolled data lets platforms and intermediaries set terms while users become inputs. A client that guards data can prove ad views or purchases cryptographically, address cohorts without identifying individuals, and demand a share of the economics.
  4. Sell ideology through felt product benefitsBrave's mission is user sovereignty, but its acquisition pitch is faster pages, lower battery drain, reduced data use, and easy Chrome migration. Consumers adopt concrete everyday improvements first; the privacy and token system can then reveal a different internet business model.
  5. Decentralization needs pragmatic user experienceSelf-custody, compliance, keys, cross-chain activity, and irreversible mistakes create anxiety that banks normally absorb. Brave combines custodial and on-chain paths, local clients and useful servers, aiming to make wallets and virtual payments routine rather than demanding ideological purity from mainstream users.
