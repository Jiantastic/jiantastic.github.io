In brief

Michael Ovitz explains how Creative Artists Agency replaced Hollywood's studio-controlled labor model with talent-controlled packages. Founded by five young William Morris agents in 1975 with $100,000, CAA used predictable television commissions to fund expansion into film. Instead of selling scripts, actors, or directors separately, it assembled complete projects and made studios compete for distribution rights. Jurassic Park became the fullest expression: Crichton, Spielberg, Kennedy, financing terms, and ownership arrived as one scarce, inseparable package.

CAA's advantage was organizational as much as contractual. Teams shared every client, information flowed through meetings, reports, readers, and the mailroom, and the agency paid generously to retain talent. That system reduced dependence on any single agent and let CAA combine clients across disciplines. Ovitz later applied the same cultural literacy and preparation to Coca-Cola advertising and the Sony and Matsushita studio acquisitions. Yet his Disney failure exposed the model's boundary: an autonomous packager who aligns independent parties may struggle inside a hierarchy controlled by another leader.

Five key insights

  1. Packaging moved leverage to creative talentCAA refused to sell material naked. By combining scripts, directors, actors, and producers before approaching a studio, it turned fragmented inputs into a unique product. Studios still supplied capital, marketing, and distribution, but could no longer swap individual creators or dictate every term.
  2. Television cash flow financed film ambitionCAA began television-only because union rules paid performers weekly, producing reliable commissions. The founders repaid their initial $100,000 debt within six months and avoided further borrowing, then used the stable base to support film projects whose development and backend economics took years.
  3. Teams converted relationships into institutional assetsTraditional agents guarded clients as personal property. CAA surrounded each client with several agents, allowing relationships to survive interpersonal friction and junior people to inherit responsibility. Shared economics and information made cross-client packages possible and helped keep losses to roughly five or six clients over 25 years.
  4. Cultural research expanded the addressable marketCAA treated magazines, art, technology, and global business as forward indicators rather than decoration. That research supported bets such as Shogun, a demographic-specific Coca-Cola campaign producing 35 commercials for the former cost of seven, and relationships that connected Japanese electronics companies with Hollywood studios.
  5. Autonomy was both strength and constraintOvitz excelled when he could align talent, money, and distribution without answering to an owner. At Disney, overlapping authority with Michael Eisner made that operating style destructive. CAA also proved difficult to diversify or capitalize because its shared culture and privately held economics resisted allocating ownership among a larger organization.
