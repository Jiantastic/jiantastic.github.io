In brief

Jason Calacanis's sprawling empire — Silicon Alley Reporter, Weblogs Inc, This Week in Startups, the Launch accelerator and syndicate — is one playbook run repeatedly: assume every new medium will reach critical mass, go all-in before anyone else, and convert media attention into power, relationships, and deal flow. From selling bootleg Empire Strikes Back tapes in his father's Brooklyn bar to becoming Sequoia's prototype Scout, each act parlays hustle into the next.

The arc is boom, crash, reinvention. He becomes king of New York media, loses everything in the dot-com bust and 9/11, rebuilds with blogs, then watches Google's Panda update gut Mahalo. Each collapse forces him to detach identity from the brand — his Bob Dylan 'don't look back' rule — while the tension between self-confessed hype man and genuinely elite returns fuels his argument that solo conviction beats partnership consensus.

Five key insights

  1. Assume every new medium reaches critical massCalacanis's stated 'big trick in life' is to behave as if each new format — zines, blogs, podcasts, Twitter — will definitely work and go full bore early. Bing paid him $30,000 for podcast ads before podcast advertising existed, and the duds (Google Plus, FriendFeed) don't matter because early hits deliver asymmetric head starts.
  2. Equity poaching, not content, beat GawkerTipped off that Gizmodo generated all of Gawker's revenue, he recruited Peter Rojas with day-one fully vested equity that Denton had only promised, then launched Engadget the first day of Denton's vacation. Weblogs Inc sold to AOL for $30 million eighteen months in, on roughly $100,000 of lifetime revenue.
  3. Platform dependency destroyed Mahalo overnightMahalo was running at $10 million in Google AdSense revenue when the Panda update removed 90% of its traffic, forcing him to lay off a hundred people. His conclusion: Google systematically neutered partners who could become competitive, and the episode is his core argument for why the company will face antitrust action.
  4. Sequoia's $700K scout capital returned $100 millionAs the first Sequoia Scout, splitting carry 50-50, he deployed $700,000 into 19 companies including Uber, Thumbtack, and DataStax — three unicorns in his first seven checks. The West Coast anointment, layered on his East Coast media credibility, is what he says made him 'dangerous.'
  5. New angels should only fund post-revenue startupsSince most startups never ship a product and most that ship never get a paying customer, restricting bets to companies with even $2,000 a month in revenue eliminates the bulk of failure risk. Small syndicate checks are his '$1 blackjack table' — tuition paid while learning, before earning the right to pre-launch bets like Robinhood.
