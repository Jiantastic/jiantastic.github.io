In brief

Ben and David argue that Microsoft's so-called lost era — from Windows 95 in 1995 to Satya Nadella's promotion in 2014 — was actually when the company won. Consumer efforts like Zune, Windows Phone, and Vista failed, yet revenue grew from $6 billion to $80 billion as Microsoft cracked the code on enterprise software and secretly built Azure, the foundation of its return to being the world's most valuable company.

The episode's arc runs from peak consumer power — crushing Netscape with a free, bundled Internet Explorer — into the DOJ antitrust trial that briefly ordered a breakup, sidelined Bill Gates, and traumatized the culture. Steve Ballmer then rebuilt Microsoft around enterprise agreements and the cloud, but the stock sat flat near $30 for his entire tenure because the company tripled profits while completely failing to tell its story.

Five key insights

  1. Bundling free Internet Explorer destroyed NetscapeNetscape sold server software on the strength of client dominance, so when Gates made IE free in every copy of Windows on December 7, 1995, Netscape's stock fell a third and never recovered. IE rode Windows distribution from zero to effectively 100% browser share by 2000.
  2. Antitrust's real cost was cultural, not legalThe 2002 settlement imposed only mild terms — API documentation, no exclusionary OEM contracts, a complaints committee — but 16 months under an active breakup order, the leaked Gates deposition tapes, and 21 years of scrutiny broke morale, changed Gates permanently, and distracted the company through the internet transition.
  3. Enterprise agreements turned software into growing annuitiesThree-year per-device EAs bundling Windows, Office, SQL Server, Exchange, and Active Directory made everything sticky without any product needing to be best-of-breed. By 2007, 55% of Microsoft's revenue flowed from EAs, versus the 29% gross margins of the abandoned retail-box model.
  4. Google's less-than-free Android doomed Windows PhoneBecause Google monetized search, it could give OEMs Android for free — even pay carriers to push it — while Microsoft asked several dollars per handset in a pennies-driven industry. The hosts trace a direct line from Google's search economics to Microsoft's inevitable mobile loss, independent of the iPhone.
  5. Azure was built under Ballmer, not SatyaRay Ozzie's 2005 Internet Services Disruption memo launched Red Dog outside the server-and-tools division, with Dave Cutler writing a hypervisor from scratch, seven years before Nadella became CEO. Ballmer funded billions in data centers against internal incentives, then handpicked Nadella to lead the cloud transition.
