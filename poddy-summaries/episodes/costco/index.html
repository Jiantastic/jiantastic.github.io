In brief

Costco is not a simple bulk retailer but roughly fifty interlocking tradeoffs — a paid membership, only 3,800 SKUs, a hard 14% markup cap, supplier-financed inventory, and high-wage labor — all tuned over decades toward one goal: the lowest possible prices on high-quality goods. Break any single piece and the machine fails; together they produce the durable, competitor-proof business Charlie Munger famously adored.

The central arc runs through Sol Price, who invented both the discounter (FedMart) and the warehouse club (Price Club) yet admitted he was better at creating businesses than running them. His protégé Jim Sinegal — a FedMart bagger who rose to run its warehouses — cloned Price Club as Costco, out-executed his mentor, and reunited the companies in the 1993 merger, fusing Price's principled model with disciplined scale execution.

Five key insights

  1. Suppliers finance Costco's inventory at standard termsInventory turns 12.4 times a year — a 26-27 day sell-through against net-30 invoices — so goods are often sold before Costco pays for them. This negative cash conversion cycle is achieved with ordinary payment terms, not by squeezing vendors.
  2. Membership fees drive 70% of operating incomeAbout $4 billion in near-100%-margin fees from 124 million members, renewing at 93% in the US, generate most of the profit on $230 billion of revenue. The prepaid fee also selects affluent shoppers ($125k median household income) and psychologically pulls them back into stores.
  3. A hard 14% markup cap compounds trustWalmart marks up around 25% and department stores 100%; Costco caps everything at 14% (15% for Kirkland). Sinegal likens raising prices to heroin, and the cap means roughly 89 cents of every dollar squeezed from a supplier passes straight to members.
  4. 3,800 SKUs unlock the entire machineAgainst Walmart's 100,000-plus SKUs, Costco's tiny catalog yields about 10x the revenue per item, giving it top-buyer leverage with nearly every supplier, enabling 92% cross-docked pallet logistics, and letting buyers pre-select quality so members accept the loss of choice.
  5. High wages make labor cheaper per dollarPaying $26 an hour versus Walmart's $19.50 produces 7% attrition (typical retail is 20%), shrinkage of just 0.15% of sales, and about $730,000 of revenue per employee — a third to half of Walmart's revenue with nearly an order of magnitude fewer people.
